# Communication Frameworks (in depth)

The seven techniques behind the [communication skill](../SKILL.md), each with what it is, why it works,
the concrete move, and a worked example. The through-line: **minimize the listener's cognitive load** —
short sentences, known concepts, clear transitions (first / then / finally).

---

## 1. Ethos / Logos / Pathos

Aristotle's three levers of persuasion. Having the right answer (logos alone) convinces almost no one.
You must activate all three.

- **Ethos — credibility.** Answers *"why should I listen to you?"* Titles, years of experience, results,
  the "white coat" effect. State it explicitly — people often hide their credibility assuming it's
  obvious, and it isn't. The *same* sentence carries far more weight from a credible source ("run slow
  to run fast" from a Sunday jogger vs. from an elite runner). Lowers skepticism so your arguments land.
- **Logos — logic.** Answers *"is it true?"* Your arguments, evidence, technical justification. Keep it
  as simple as possible: the higher the cognitive load, the more listeners drop off. Use short sentences,
  known concepts, explicit transitions. Beware **sophisms** (intentional fake syllogisms) and
  **paralogisms** (accidental ones) — speech that *sounds* logical but means nothing; learn to spot and
  name them, both to defend yourself and to avoid producing them.
- **Pathos — emotion.** Answers *"will I remember?"* Creates engagement that (a) captures attention,
  (b) makes the message memorable, (c) drives action. Tools: a personal anecdote, an example, a
  metaphor, humor, and **numbers staged inside a story** — a narrative is recalled ~22× better than a
  bare figure (Stanford). We remember what carried emotion (where you were on a major event vs. the
  next ordinary day).

**Delivery order: ethos → logos → pathos.** Install trust, structure and prove, then anchor emotionally
and trigger action.

> Without ethos, your words are easily challenged. With ethos + logos you get the "expert discourse":
> true and deep, but neither agreeable nor memorable — which is exactly why pathos is the finishing brick.

**Worked example — "the value of pauses when speaking":**
- Ethos: *"I've coached hundreds of speakers, and the pause is the highest-ROI change."*
- Logos: *"A study found pauses raise audience attention by ~33%."*
- Pathos: *"You know when you're talking and you feel nobody's listening? Pause, look them in the eye —
  it flips instantly. It's like dropping a bucket of ice water on someone dozing off."*

**Exercise to give the user:** next time they speak, do it in order — establish experience (ethos),
give the reasoning (logos), then show the impact / what they gain or avoid (pathos).

## 2. Reframing (recadrage)

For trap or destabilizing questions that catch you off guard ("why are you more expensive than your
competitors?"). Surprise makes rebuttal hard; reframing seizes the question and turns the framing to
your advantage — **without dodging.** Stay relevant and in-theme.

**Three-step framing method:**
1. **Receive** — acknowledge without justifying. Lowers tension. *"Good question — we get it a lot."*
2. **Bridge** — a pivot phrase linking acknowledgment to redirection. *"…and there's an even more
   important angle here."* Don't reveal the point yet.
3. **New frame** — pose the reframed question that moves the conversation onto better ground and makes
   *them* talk. *"The real question might be what they do less of to be cheaper."*

**Variant — answer a question with a question:** *"Which part of what I described feels expensive?"* /
*"What makes it feel costly to you?"*

> The line between reframing and **diversion**: reframing still answers and stays on-theme; diversion
> abandons it. Reframe, don't dodge — otherwise you're just an impostor.

## 3. Focus on the other (not yourself)

The most common sabotage: talking too much about *yourself / your idea / your solution*. Usually
unintentional (passion), but from the outside it's striking how it loses the room — listeners wait
politely for their turn while thinking *"how does this concern me?"*. Top communicators do the inverse:
they verbalize the **other person's problem first**, sometimes better than the person could.

Moving the spotlight onto the listener also moves *your own stress* off yourself — and the person feels
understood, which is what actually builds credibility (more than having the best arguments).

**Three preparation questions before an important exchange:**
1. **What is their problem?** (shift focus from you to them)
2. **How does it concretely manifest for them?** (pressure? risk? wasted time? stress?)
3. **How do I address it in what I say?** (what can I do to help, within my power?)

Done sincerely, you no longer need attention "tricks" — you have a genuinely relevant base.

## 4. Pyramid Principle

Barbara Minto's method (McKinsey; standard at BCG/McKinsey). For executives and structured, time-poor
people, your job is **not** the detail — it's getting to the essential so they can decide.

**Conclusion first, proof second.** Start at the top (the decision/recommendation), then summarize *why*
in ~3 points, then drill into detail **only if asked.**

This reverses your *thinking* order (collect data → cluster → conclude) for your *delivery* order
(conclude → cluster → detail-on-demand). Leading with the conclusion is a hallmark of **assertive**
communication — the most effective professional mode.

**Worked example:** *"We need to double the budget."* (the ask, immediately) → *"Because: we're behind
and must catch up; resource constraints; high-impact opportunity."* (3 high-level proofs) → then drill:
*"behind by how much? why?"* in the detail layer.

> The classic failure: the speaker walks through context → history → findings → and only reaches the
> recommendation at the end, by which point the exec has already asked "where are you going with this?"

## 5. Pattern interrupt

Attention spans have collapsed; holding people even in an ordinary conversation is harder and more
important (remote meetings especially — same context, same slides, same tone → multitasking). The fix:
**break the pattern** to shake people back — *without making them feel attacked.*

**The menu:**
- An **unexpected / counter-intuitive line** ("luxury is completely outdated" — said by someone in luxury).
- A **long silence** (longer than usual).
- A **blunt, direct question**.
- A single one-word answer, then wait.
- An **out-of-place image** (a dancer's photo in a business meeting).
- Taking the topic from an angle no one expects.

> Requires substance behind it. A "hook" with nothing to follow makes you look like a clown — after the
> interrupt you must deliver (often the logos layer). Use sparingly.

## 6. Make it safe (de-risk the idea)

People often resist not from lack of conviction but from **perceived risk** — saying yes and being wrong
is personal/political exposure (this is why "change management" exists). Effective communicators get
others to adopt a different position without that risk being felt. It's fundamentally **empathy**:
understand the perceived risk and transform a threatening idea into a merely *conceivable* one before
suggesting it.

**Three steps:**
1. **Normalize hesitation** — don't blame people for doubting. *"This is something we've never done, so
   it's normal to wonder if it'll work."*
2. **Reduce the risk** — make it reversible/small. *"We can start with a pilot and roll back if it
   doesn't work — nothing irreversible."*
3. **Protect the person** — not just the idea. Show that following it won't endanger them or their team.

Net: **de-risk the idea, make the step accessible, reduce cognitive load.** Safe + incremental drops
many objections before they're raised. When someone feels safe, they become far more open to change.

## 7. The 7-38-55 (Mehrabian) myth

The popular claim — communication is 7% words / 38% tone / 55% body language, attributed to psychologist
Albert Mehrabian (UCLA) — is **misunderstood**, not a general law.

Those figures came from a **narrow lab setup**: a *single word or tiny phrase* (not full sentences),
where the listener was trying to **guess an emotion**, specifically to **detect a contradiction** — e.g.
someone saying "super" in a tone that belies the word. *Only there* do tone/face outweigh the word.

In the vast majority of real communication, **words are essential** and the proportions don't hold.
**Takeaway:** verbal, paraverbal, and nonverbal *all* matter — what you say and how you say it carry
comparable weight. Don't obsess over exact percentages; just know all three count.

---

## Conclusion

It's not the best ideas that win — it's the ones **communicated best**. That can feel unfair, but it's
the observable reality. The point of these techniques is to close that gap.

**Coaching close:** have the user pick **one** technique and commit to *when* they'll apply it next.
Watching/reading without applying is wasted time.
